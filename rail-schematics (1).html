Rail Safety V.01

Early-stage concept for AI-assisted rail car anomaly detection. Two architectures explored: a stationary trackside sensor unit and an on-car unit. Both are concept sketches with open problems and reasoning preserved.

Schematic 01

Rail Unit — Trackside / Stationary

Most freight rail accidents originate from detectable mechanical failure before the event: dragging equipment, overheated bearings, structural anomaly. This concept places a fixed sensor array at track level to passively inspect every passing car. No installation on rolling stock required, which makes it easier to prototype, test and iterate. The core challenge is associating sensor readings to individual cars reliably, and doing so robustly across variable lighting and weather.

Schematic 02

On-Car Unit

The on-car unit addresses the trackside system's core limitation: it can only inspect cars when they pass a fixed point. An onboard sensor package would monitor continuously, picking up every grade change, every coupler impact, every mile of track. More powerful in theory, but it immediately runs into a cluster of practical problems around power, networking, and data collection. Pivoted away from after working through the open problems below.

Pivot reasoning Starting with a stationary sensor is more practical: easier to gather training data, nothing needs to be installed on rolling stock, and the iteration cycle is faster. The goal is to validate the sensor combination and models at the trackside level first, then move toward on-car units once that foundation exists. See: Schematic 01.

Next step if pursued: confirm the CV, thermometric and vibration sensor combination at track level before any on-car work.
